Act I

A technology
still pricing itself.

Nobody agrees on what a solid-state cell costs today — and that uncertainty is the single most important fact for anyone underwriting this transition.

Li-ion has a Tier 1 price anchor. Solid-state does not.

Conventional lithium-ion hit a record-low $139/kWh, then $132/kWh the following year (BloombergNEF, tier 1). Solid-state has no equivalent single authoritative figure — only a scatter of disagreeing estimates.

Act III

Old chokepoints,
new rules.

Solid-state doesn't escape the geopolitics of the battery supply chain — it inherits it, plus a layer of chemistry-specific catch-up regulation.

China's concentration is the starting condition

~80%Battery supply-chain share

Across many segments; ≥95% in precursor and LFP cathode materials [needs verification, tier 2].

97–98%Synthetic graphite anode market

Described as a deliberate export-control chokepoint (Carnegie Endowment, tier 2, corroborated).

70%Global cobalt from the DRC

New export-quota regime from 16 Oct 2025: 96,600-tonne annual ceiling for 2026–27, ~48% of 2024 production.

The one hedge

Lithium-metal-anode solid-state designs are framed by Carnegie as a partial long-term mitigant — projecting reduced graphite (~25% by 2035) and cobalt (~10% by 2035) dependence. A forward-looking hedge, not present-day relief: today's lithium-price volatility (roughly doubling in Q1 2026) still bites solid-state economics as hard as conventional Li-ion.

Export controls have sharpened — and gone extraterritorial

China's Ministry of Commerce/Customs imposed new controls effective 8 November 2025 on lithium-ion batteries ≥300 Wh/kg, manufacturing equipment, LFP cathode materials, and graphite anode tech — with extraterritorial reach from 1 December 2025 at a 0.1% de minimis threshold, explicitly modeled on the US's own 29 September 2025 expansion (tier 1, corroborated by Mayer Brown analysis). On the US side, Section 30D "Foreign Entity of Concern" rules bar Clean Vehicle Credit eligibility for FEOC battery components (2024) and FEOC critical minerals (2025) — final rules took effect 5 July 2024. No solid-state-specific FEOC guidance exists yet; general Li-ion rules are presumed to apply, but this is inference, not confirmed policy [needs verification].

EXHIBIT 02Standards & safety — certified under existing rules, built for a different failure mode

UN 38.3 remains the mandatory international transport-safety regime — eight tests, unchanged for solid-state cells despite different failure modes. Factorial announced UN 38.3 certification for a 100Ah lithium-metal solid-state cell (tier 1 company release) — a single-company claim, not a general regulatory finding.

China published GB/T 43568-2026, the world's first national standard for automotive solid-state batteries. [Conflict flagged]: one Feb-2026 source describes a comment period ending 28 Feb with approval expected April; a later report presents it as already in force from 1 July 2026. Plausibly consistent, but no Tier 1 government-gazette source independently confirms the exact date.

EU Battery Regulation (EU) 2023/1542: 65% recycling efficiency for lithium-based batteries by end-2025, rising to 70% by end-2030 (tier 1). No solid-state-specific recycling regulation (sulfide/ceramic handling, H₂S risk) was found — flagged as a likely genuine regulatory gap, not a search artifact.

Act IV

The engineering
still being solved.

Under every roadmap date sits a set of unresolved materials-science and manufacturing problems — three competing electrolyte chemistries, a dendrite problem, and a stack-pressure cost burden.

Sulfide vs. oxide vs. polymer — the chemistry that drives who partners with whom

01Sulfide

Highest ionic conductivity, closest to liquid electrolytes. Requires dry-room/inert processing (air/moisture-sensitive). Dominant automotive path: Toyota, Samsung SDI, CATL, Solid Power, BMW.

02Oxide

Widest electrochemical stability window, chemically robust. Requires high-temp sintering; hard to produce thin, defect-free layers at scale. Notably less automotive-dominant today.

03Polymer

Lower energy density but most scalable today — compatible with roll-to-roll Li-ion-like processes. Underpins already-commercial semi-SSBs, at the cost of needing 60–90°C to reach usable conductivity.

This comparison rests primarily on a Tier 3 source used as a lead only [needs verification] — the IEA's Tier 1 Semi-/Almost-/All-SSB framing corroborates only the high-level structure, not these specific figures.

Dendrites, interfacial resistance, and stack pressure

800

Cycles at ~25°C that QuantumScape's ceramic separator is reported to resist dendrite formation at automotive-relevant power densities (tier 1, corroborated by SEC filing).

Its "Cobra" manufacturing process claims ~25× faster/more compact production than the prior method.

Manufacturing scale-up is where the remaining hard problems concentrate (source: manufacturing-scaleup-challenges.md — reviewed clean, no flags). Interfacial resistance between electrode and solid electrolyte is the central obstacle distinguishing solid-state from liquid-electrolyte cells (Springer, tier 1 peer-reviewed); for dry-processed electrodes, residual porosity is the primary driver of that resistance. A Nature Communications co-rolling technique achieved >80% capacity retention after 500 cycles at just 2 MPa stack pressure — substantially below non-co-rolled equivalents (tier 1 peer-reviewed). Yet BMW/Solid Power's own i7 prototype program still lists "managing cell expansion, regulating operating pressure, and optimizing temperature" as active test focus as of May 2026 (tier 1 official BMW) — proof this remains unresolved even at automotive validation stage.

No oxide-electrolyte data

Available stack-pressure and interfacial-resistance figures are sulfide-oriented only — no equivalent data found for oxide-electrolyte systems specifically. A genuine coverage gap.

Charging speed and safety — claims vs. verified data

Industry claims cluster around single-digit-minute 10–80% fast charging on 800–1,200V architectures, but the strongest concrete figures are OEM-stated targets (Toyota's ~10 minutes) rather than independently verified third-party results. Trade-press claims of "80% in 4.5 minutes" are Tier 2/3 vendor claims pending independent verification. On safety, the core rationale — solid electrolytes are non-flammable, removing a key thermal-runaway pathway — is a structural chemistry argument, not an independently quantified comparative figure [needs verification]; no dedicated third-party comparative safety study was surfaced in this research pass.

Two cross-cutting caveats

Every "commercial launch" year is a roadmap commitment, not a guarantee — Toyota's repeated slippage is the proof case, and no comparable public delay history yet exists for the others. Cost-per-kWh targets remain undisclosed at the program level for VW/PowerCo, BMW, and Stellantis alike, reinforcing Act I's point: the economics of this transition are the least-answered question in the entire field.

In summary

What would have
to be true.

The demonstrations are real and increasingly public. The commercial proof points that would resolve the central questions are still, without exception, in the future tense.

01When, not if — but which "when."

Every automaker roadmap date is a commitment, not a guarantee. Toyota's own history (2020→23→26→27–28) is the industry's cautionary tale. The Toyota 2027-vs-2028 and Nissan 2028-vs-2029 conflicts are preserved, not resolved.

02At what cost.

Cost estimates disagree by roughly 2× before accounting for outliers. Only Nissan has disclosed a $/kWh target ($75/kWh by 2028, unverified). Cost parity with Li-ion is projected anywhere from the early-2030s to 2035.

03Whether it's solved, or just contained in a lab.

Dendrite control and stack pressure show promising peer-reviewed results, but BMW's own i7 program still lists pressure/thermal management as active engineering work in mid-2026. Independently verified charging-speed data at pack scale remains thin.

None of this undercuts the strategic logic for automakers pursuing solid-state — the energy-density and range upside is real and independently demonstrated in at least one case (the EQS road trip). But for an exec audience weighing capital allocation or competitive timing: this is a technology with a genuine physics-and-engineering case, a still-unresolved cost structure, and a set of launch dates that history suggests should be discounted, not taken at face value.
